GRIZZLY BEAR

The grizzly bear (Ursus arctos horribilis) is a large and powerful subspecies of the brown bear native to North America. Recognizable by its distinctive hump on its shoulders and brown fur that can range from light tan to almost black, the grizzly is an omnivore with a varied diet including berries, roots, fish, small mammals, and carrion. These bears are solitary animals, except for mothers with cubs, and are known for their strength and endurance. Grizzlies inhabit forests, alpine meadows, and tundra regions, often near rivers and streams. While their populations have been reduced by habitat loss and hunting, conservation efforts have helped stabilize some populations, but they remain protected under various regulations.

FACTS

  • COMMON NAME: GRIZZLY BEAR
  • SCIENTIFIC NAME: URSUS ARCTOS HORRIBILIS
  • TYPE: MAMMALS
  • SIZE: 6.5 TO 8 FEET (Length)
  • WEIGHT: 400 TO 790 POUNDS
  • AVERAGE LIFE SPAN: 20 TO 25 YEARS
